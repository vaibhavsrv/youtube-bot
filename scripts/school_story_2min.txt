School story — ~2 minutes voiceover (about 240–280 words at a calm pace)
Title on screen (you add in edit): “The New Kid Brought the Entourage”

--- HOOK ~0:00–0:20 ---
Every school has a quiet kid in the back row. At Lincoln High, that was Marcus—hoodie up, headphones in, never raised his hand. People assumed he was shy. They were half right. He was trying very hard not to be noticed… which is difficult when two black SUVs idle outside the gym every afternoon.

--- BEAT 1 ~0:20–0:45 ---
Fact one: rich kids don’t always look rich. Marcus wore the same faded sneakers for a month. Meanwhile, the vice principal called him “sir” by accident in the hallway and corrected himself like it mattered. The lunch lady gave him an extra slice of pizza. Small stuff. Weird stuff. The kind of stuff you only notice if you’re paying attention.

--- BEAT 2 ~0:45–1:10 ---
Fact two: rumors move faster than truth. Someone said Marcus was a diplomat’s son. Someone else swore he was in witness protection. The truth was simpler and stranger: his family ran a company half the world used without knowing the name. Marcus wanted one normal semester. Just one.

--- BEAT 3 ~1:10–1:35 ---
Fact three: high school hierarchy breaks when fear shows up. When Tyler cornered Marcus behind the bleachers, three men in suits appeared so smoothly it felt like a cutscene. They didn’t shout. They didn’t touch Tyler. They just stood there—calm, professional, unavoidable. Tyler apologized to the air. Marcus looked embarrassed for everyone involved.

--- BEAT 4 ~1:35–1:55 ---
Fact four: secrets get heavy. Marcus started eating lunch on the roof with Ava, the only person who asked direct questions without wanting money or clout. She didn’t care about the SUVs. She cared that he flinched when a door slammed. “You can be important and still be scared,” she said. For the first time, Marcus laughed for real.

--- CLOSER ~1:55–2:10 ---
Fact five: “normal” is a moving target. By finals week, the SUVs were still there—but farther away. Marcus joined one club. He lost a basketball game on purpose so nobody would overthink it. And the school learned a lesson that sounds fake but isn’t: sometimes the quiet kid isn’t hiding from you. He’s hiding from everything that follows him when the bell rings.

--- END ---
Optional last line (shorter run time): “And honestly? Lincoln High never looked at the parking lot the same way again.”
